MPI Background

MPI scores represent the best operationalized non-income based approach to measuring poverty. It measures acute poverty, including those who are poor due to absolute deprivation in one necessity and those who experience multiple forms of deprivation that result in poverty. This approach captures the spirit of Amartya Sen’s idea of shifting from income-based poverty metrics to a capabilities understanding of poverty and deprivation. For an overview of the issues with income-based poverty metrics and the importance of other measures, see Laderchi, Saith, and Stewart (2003).
MPIs measure both incidence and intensity of poverty, combining these measurements to understand severity of poverty in an area. MPI scores are calculated by measuring the incidence and intensity of ten indicators of poverty:

Figure 1: MPI Dimensions of Poverty


This methodology allows inter- and intracountry comparisons of the intensity of poverty, capturing more information than simple PPP thresholds. See OPI’s explanation for further detail.

Now we’ll look at countries with the worst intra-country regional differences. Here, we’re interested in looking within countries to identify those who have severe income inequality. To do so we use sub-national data to find the difference between the highest and lowest MPIs within a country. 

max <- subnational %>% 
  group_by(Country) %>%
  filter(MPI_Regional == max(MPI_Regional)) %>%
  dplyr::select(ISO_country_code, Country, Sub_national_region, MPI_Regional, MPI_National) %>% 
  rename(Highest_Region = Sub_national_region, Highest_MPI = MPI_Regional)
min <- subnational %>% 
  group_by(Country) %>%
  filter(MPI_Regional == min(MPI_Regional)) %>%
  dplyr::select(ISO_country_code, Country, Sub_national_region, MPI_Regional, MPI_National) %>% 
  rename(Lowest_Region = Sub_national_region, Lowest_MPI = MPI_Regional)
gaps <- merge(max, min) %>% 
  mutate(MPI_Gap = Highest_MPI - Lowest_MPI)

gaps %>%
  mutate(Rank = min_rank(-MPI_Gap)) %>%

  arrange(Rank) %>% 
  head(10) %>% 
  kable() %>%

Conclusion

Ultimately, this analysis has shown that there are significant intra- and intercountry differences in poverty. This underscores that poverty must not be considered simply a national battle. Poverty reduction relies on understanding regional differences and working with local actors to plan interventions. As Robert Chambers (1995) describes, this process relies on development practitioners listening to local realities and shifting accountability out of development centers.
